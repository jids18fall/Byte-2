Quality: About This Data Set

This dataset is not complete, due to the difficulties in collecting early samples. So, there are some missing fields in some records. What's more, the summary statistics of text fields and distributions of numerical fields all match our expectations. Therefore, the dataset is coherent. Finally, through the check of counting medals amount in different sports, the data should be correct. However, the unbalance amounts of records in different sports may make it difficult to explore general conclusions in this dataset.

Is the data complete?

If not, what is missing and what concerns does it raise? What is your evidence for this?

No. Some fields of a small part of the records are missing. In general, some of the ages, heights and weights are missing. A histogram of the missing values in each column is shown below.

To investigate the reasons of these missing values, we generate the histogram of the 'Year' field of the records with missing values. The histogram is shown below.

From this histogram, we know that the majority of the missing values come from early records. It is reasonble because some records of the athletes might loss, and the data management technology at that days was not as good as nowadays. Rows with missing data are deleted before the further data processing, because it will be difficult to generate alternative data for these fields.

After the deletion, the remaining data should not be considered complete. Since more early records than recent records are deleted, it might introduce biases in the interpretation.

Is the data correct?

What aspects of this data can you verify? How did you do so? What did you find?

The data itself should be correct, because there will be official records of the Olympic participants. However, due to the missing values, the conclusion drawn from this data might be biased, as shown in the section discussing the completeness of the data.

In addition, the difference in the sample sizes of different sports might also make it difficult to investigate a general conclusion. The below histogram shows the number of Gold medalists in different sports.

Directly using all records to explore the global structure may lead to the Simpson's Paradox, when there is a large unbalance between different groups of data. The histogram of Silver medalists is shown below.

And the histogram of Bronze medalists is also shown below.

As shown by the three histograms above, the distributions are similar. Therefore, in terms of the medal counts in different sports, the data should be correct.
